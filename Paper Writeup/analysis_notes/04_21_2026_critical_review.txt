OVERALL VERDICT: REJECT (MAJOR REVISION REQUIRED)

The paper addresses a timely and important problem---PQC migration for V2X
safety communications. The theoretical TBS feasibility analysis (Section 4.6)
is rigorous and constitutes a genuine contribution. However, the empirical
evaluation (Sections 5-7) contains fundamental data integrity problems,
methodological gaps, and unsupported claims that would not survive peer review
at a reputable venue. The problems below are ordered by severity.

PROBLEMS:

(a) THREE SCENARIOS HAVE PDR > 100%: ECDSA B-LOS (233.65%) and Falcon D-LOS
    (173.68%) report more packets received than sent. This is impossible and
    indicates a bug in the data collection pipeline (likely duplicate counting
    of received packets, or the sender_summary.csv not capturing all senders).
    The paper acknowledges this for Density D but silently excludes ECDSA B-LOS
    without disclosing it.

(b) THREE SIMULATIONS CRASHED BEFORE 300s: ECDSA B-LOS (124s), Falcon D-LOS
    (125s), and Falcon F-LOS (81s) terminated early. Falcon F ran for only 27%
    of the target duration. The paper does not disclose these crashes or discuss
    how truncated data was handled.

(c) VEHICLE COUNTS ARE WILDLY INCONSISTENT ACROSS "MATCHED" SCENARIOS:
    - ECDSA D-LOS has 972 unique senders; Falcon D-LOS has 67. That's a 14.5x
      difference for scenarios labeled "40 veh/km."
    - ECDSA B-LOS has 45 senders; Falcon B-LOS has 492. An 11x difference.
    - The paper presents ECDSA D-LOS (57.43% PDR) in Table III alongside
      Falcon D-LOS data, implying they are comparable. They are not.

(d) TOTAL_SENT MISMATCH BREAKS PDR COMPARISON: Valid matched comparisons
    (identical Total_Sent) exist for ONLY 3 scenario pairs:
    - A-LOS:  ECDSA=202,631 vs Falcon=202,631  (VALID)
    - A-NLOS: ECDSA=202,631 vs Falcon=202,631  (VALID)
    - B-NLOS: ECDSA=455,861 vs Falcon=455,861  (VALID)
    Every other comparison in the paper compares different vehicle populations,
    rendering the PDR delta meaningless.

IMPACT: The paper's primary empirical claim --- that Falcon imposes a 2-3 pp
PDR penalty --- rests on exactly 3 valid comparisons, all at low densities
(A and B-NLOS). The density sweep, scalability assessment, and high-density
conclusions are built on incomparable or corrupted data.

FIX: Discard all crashed/corrupted scenarios. Re-run D, E, F with both
algorithms using identical SUMO route files and verified sim-time-limit=300s.
Until this is done, Table III and Figure 5 are unreliable.


---------------------------------------------------------------------------
F2: NO REPLICATIONS, NO CONFIDENCE INTERVALS (SEVERITY: FATAL)
---------------------------------------------------------------------------

Every scenario was run exactly once with a single random seed. There are no
replications, no error bars, no confidence intervals, and no statistical
significance tests anywhere in the paper.

Wireless channel simulation is inherently stochastic: SPS scheduling, Rayleigh
fading, vehicle arrival timing, and resource selection all involve randomness.
A single run can produce atypical results due to:
  - Favorable/unfavorable vehicle spatial distributions
  - Lucky/unlucky SPS grant selections
  - Transient warm-up effects dominating short simulations

The "non-monotonic anomaly" at Density E (PDR jumping from 53% to 84%) is
Exhibit A: a single-run artifact. Without replications, it's impossible to
distinguish genuine algorithmic behavior from random fluctuation.

MINIMUM STANDARD: Any reputable venue (IEEE TVT, TITS, VTC, VNC) requires:
  - At minimum 5 independent replications per scenario (different random seeds)
  - Mean +/- 95% confidence interval for every reported metric
  - Statistical significance test (e.g., Welch's t-test) for PDR differences

FIX: Run at least 5 replications per scenario with different seeds.
Report mean +/- 95% CI. Apply paired t-tests to ECDSA vs Falcon PDR deltas.


---------------------------------------------------------------------------
F3: DATA ONLY CAPTURES RSU RECEPTION, NOT V2V (SEVERITY: FATAL)
---------------------------------------------------------------------------

The CSV "receiver" column contains only "rsu[0]" --- every logged packet was
received by the roadside unit. There is NO vehicle-to-vehicle (V2V) reception
data in the dataset.

But the paper:
  - Title says "C-V2X Sidelink Communications" (implies V2V)
  - Claims PDR is "computed across all vehicle pairs within communication range"
  - Evaluates "V2V safety applications" like forward collision warning
  - References SAE J2945/1, which specifies V2V BSM delivery requirements

What is actually measured is V2I (vehicle-to-infrastructure) PDR at a single
RSU. V2I PDR is NOT the same as V2V PDR because:
  - The RSU is at a fixed location (intersection center), not distributed
  - Hidden-node effects differ for RSU vs distributed vehicle receivers
  - V2V communication range and geometry differ from V2I

The paper must either:
(a) Acknowledge that only V2I reception is measured and reframe all claims
    accordingly, OR
(b) Modify the simulator to log V2V reception and re-run all scenarios.

Option (a) is feasible but significantly weakens the paper's contribution,
since V2V is the primary use case for Mode 4 sidelink.

SECTION 2: MAJOR WEAKNESSES (Each significantly weakens the paper)
================================================================================


---------------------------------------------------------------------------
W1: CLAIMED KPI (CBR) NEVER ANALYZED (SEVERITY: HIGH)
---------------------------------------------------------------------------

Section 4.7 (KPIs) explicitly lists CBR as a key performance indicator and
explains why it matters (congestion control triggering, DCC drops). The
abstract promises CBR evaluation: "channel busy ratio (CBR), and resource
utilization, are compared across PQC algorithms."

But Sections 5-7 contain:
  - Zero CBR figures
  - Zero CBR tables
  - Zero CBR numerical values
  - Zero DCC analysis

This is a broken promise to the reader. CBR is arguably the STRONGEST argument
for PQC impact (the earlier review noted Falcon CBR ~2.5x higher than ECDSA),
yet it's completely absent from results.

FIX: Add CBR time-series plots, CBR vs density comparison, and DCC drop counts.
This data may already exist in .sca/.vec files even if not in the CSV.


---------------------------------------------------------------------------
W2: PAPER TABLE II SAYS 60s SIMULATION, ACTUAL DATA IS 300s (SEVERITY: HIGH)
---------------------------------------------------------------------------

Table II (Simulation Parameters) states "Simulation duration: 60s". But the
actual simulation data shows runs ending at 256-300s, and omnetpp.ini has
sim-time-limit = 300s for all named configs.

This is either:
(a) A documentation error (Table II is outdated), or
(b) The table is correct and some results used 60s while others used 300s

Either way, the paper misrepresents its own experimental setup. If it's (a),
fix the table. If it's (b), the inconsistency invalidates cross-scenario
comparisons.


---------------------------------------------------------------------------
W3: NO DISTANCE-FILTERED PDR (SEVERITY: HIGH)
---------------------------------------------------------------------------

V2X performance is standardly evaluated at specific distances:
  - SAE J2945/1: 300m communication range
  - CHASM (Twardokus 2025): reports PDR at 100m, 300m, 500m intervals
  - Most Mode 4 papers: PDR vs distance curves

The paper reports only aggregate PDR across ALL distances. This masks the
distance-dependent behavior (PDR degrades with distance) and makes results
incomparable to the existing literature. The raw data contains a "dist_m"
column that could support this analysis.

FIX: Plot PDR vs distance in 50m bins. Report PDR at 300m specifically.
The data already supports this; only the analysis script needs to be extended.


---------------------------------------------------------------------------
W4: NO QUANTITATIVE COMPARISON WITH CHASM (SEVERITY: HIGH)
---------------------------------------------------------------------------

The paper positions itself against CHASM (Twardokus & Rahbari, IEEE TITS
2025), the closest related work. But the comparison remains qualitative only:
  - No side-by-side PDR numbers at comparable densities
  - No discussion of why PDR differs from CHASM's results
  - The "first empirical evidence" claim in the conclusion directly conflicts
    with CHASM's existence

The related work section cites CHASM but doesn't engage with its findings.
A reviewer from the CHASM group would be particularly hostile to this gap.

FIX: Add a comparison table showing CHASM's reported metrics vs this study's,
with clear explanation of scenario differences. Drop the "first" claim, or
qualify it precisely (e.g., "first evaluation using openCV2X framework").


---------------------------------------------------------------------------
W5: 20 MHz BANDWIDTH UNDERMINES ALL QUANTITATIVE CLAIMS (SEVERITY: HIGH)
---------------------------------------------------------------------------

SAE J3161 specifies 10 MHz (50 RBs, 5 subchannels). The simulation uses
20 MHz (100 RBs, 10 subchannels). This doubles available resources and halves
contention. The paper acknowledges this as a limitation (Section 6.4) but
does not quantify the impact.

The consequences are severe:
  - At 10 MHz, Falcon digest-mode requires 4 of 5 subchannels (80% occupancy),
    not 4 of 10 (40%). This means a SINGLE Falcon transmission monopolizes
    80% of the channel.
  - Falcon cert-mode (8 subchannels) is physically IMPOSSIBLE at 10 MHz
    (only 5 subchannels available), making Falcon infeasible for cert
    transmissions under the actual J3161 spec!
  - All PDR results are optimistic by roughly a factor that would push Falcon
    into infeasible territory even at light densities.

This isn't just a limitation --- it potentially invalidates the core conclusion
that "Falcon-512 is conditionally viable." At 10 MHz, Falcon may not be viable
at ANY density.

FIX: Either (a) re-run critical scenarios at 10 MHz, or (b) add explicit
analytical estimates of 10 MHz performance and acknowledge that Falcon cert
mode is infeasible at 10 MHz. Option (b) is minimum; option (a) is preferred.


---------------------------------------------------------------------------
W6: SIGN/VERIFY TIME NOT REPORTED (SEVERITY: MEDIUM)
---------------------------------------------------------------------------

The paper claims "real-time signing and verification of every BSM, capturing
actual computational overhead in the simulation timing." The data contains
columns for these measurements, but ZERO sign/verify time data is presented
in the results. This is a missed opportunity and a broken implicit promise.

FIX: Add a table or figure showing mean sign/verify times per algorithm.
The data is already captured; it just needs to be extracted and presented.


---------------------------------------------------------------------------
W7: DENSITY E "ANOMALY" IS EXPLAINABLE, NOT ANOMALOUS (SEVERITY: MEDIUM)
---------------------------------------------------------------------------

The paper labels Density E's 84.44% PDR as "anomalous" (Section 5.4). But
data inspection reveals a simple explanation: Falcon E-LOS has only 232 unique
senders vs Falcon B-LOS's 492 senders. Despite being labeled "50 veh/km" vs
"20 veh/km", Density E actually had FEWER unique transmitting vehicles than
Density B. The PDR is higher because there was less contention.

Additionally, max concurrent vehicles at the end of Density E was 800, but
with only 232 unique senders in total over the 256s duration, many of these
vehicles may have entered and exited the simulation area, never all
transmitting simultaneously.

This suggests the SUMO route files for different densities are not properly
calibrated to produce the labeled densities. The "density" labels in the paper
may not reflect actual density in the simulation.

FIX: Verify that SUMO actually produces the claimed densities. Report actual
instantaneous vehicle counts over time, not just nominal labels.


---------------------------------------------------------------------------
W8: MISSING ECDSA DATA AT HIGH DENSITIES (SEVERITY: MEDIUM)
---------------------------------------------------------------------------

The scalability assessment (Section 5.4, Figure 5) shows Falcon-only PDR at
densities A, B, E, F. But there is NO ECDSA baseline at densities E or F.
Without an ECDSA comparison, the reader cannot distinguish:
  - PDR degradation due to Falcon's overhead, vs
  - PDR degradation that would occur with ANY algorithm at those densities

The paper draws conclusions about Falcon's "infeasibility" at high densities
without showing whether ECDSA also degrades. If ECDSA also drops to 30% PDR
at density F, then the problem is congestion, not PQC overhead.

FIX: Run ECDSA at densities E and F with the same SUMO configs used for
Falcon. This is the most important missing comparison.

SECTION 3: MODERATE WEAKNESSES (Should be fixed before submission)
================================================================================


---------------------------------------------------------------------------
M1: CERTIFICATE CACHING IMPACT NOT ANALYZED (SEVERITY: MEDIUM)
---------------------------------------------------------------------------

The data distinguishes signerType=0 (digest, 745B) vs signerType=1 (cert,
1739B) for Falcon. Certificate caching is a crucial mitigation strategy ---
if most transmissions use digest mode, the effective overhead is much lower.
But the paper never analyzes:
  - The actual cert-vs-digest split in practice
  - Whether different cert inclusion intervals affect PDR
  - What happens in a hypothetical "always digest" mode

This would strengthen the feasibility argument considerably.


---------------------------------------------------------------------------
M2: ACTUAL SPDU SIZES DON'T MATCH PAPER CLAIMS (SEVERITY: LOW)
---------------------------------------------------------------------------

Table III (SPDU Sizes): ECDSA = ~301B, Falcon = ~1735B
Actual logged data:      ECDSA = 305B (cert) / 143B (digest)
                         Falcon = 1739B (cert) / 745B (digest)

The 4B discrepancy (301 vs 305, 1735 vs 1739) is because the paper assumes
BSM payload = 39B but the simulation uses bsm_data_size = 43B. Minor, but
the paper should report actual sizes from the simulation, not theoretical.


---------------------------------------------------------------------------
M3: BASELINE ECDSA PDR IS SUSPICIOUSLY LOW (SEVERITY: MEDIUM)
---------------------------------------------------------------------------

ECDSA achieves only 72% PDR at 10 veh/km (LOS). In comparable literature:
  - Gonzalez-Martin et al. (2019): >95% PDR at similar density
  - McCarthy et al. (2020, openCV2X): ~90% PDR at 80 vehicles
  - CHASM (2025): >85% PDR under similar conditions

A 72% PDR at the lightest density suggests something is wrong with the
simulation setup beyond PQC overhead. Possible causes:
  - 20 MHz bandwidth (should help, not hurt) --- not the explanation
  - Missing one-shot transmission (J3161 Section 7.3.3) --- acknowledged
  - RSU-only reception capturing a subset of possible receptions
  - Bug in the data collection pipeline
  - Overly aggressive NLOS model being applied even in "LOS" scenarios

If the baseline is broken, relative comparisons may still hold, but absolute
PDR values cannot be trusted. The paper should explicitly benchmark ECDSA PDR
against published openCV2X results to validate the baseline.


---------------------------------------------------------------------------
M4: SINGLE-INTERSECTION TOPOLOGY (SEVERITY: MEDIUM)
---------------------------------------------------------------------------

All results are from a single 4-leg intersection. This produces:
  - Maximum spatial concentration of transmitters
  - Worst-case hidden-node conditions
  - No evaluation of highway scenarios (the primary Mode 4 use case)

Highway configs exist in the simulation but were never run. At least one
highway scenario would significantly broaden the paper's applicability.

================================================================================
SECTION 5: STRENGTHS (These should be preserved and emphasized)
================================================================================


S1: REAL CRYPTOGRAPHIC OPERATIONS
    Unlike most V2X security studies that model crypto as fixed delays, this
    work uses actual liboqs/OpenSSL signing and verification. This is a genuine
    methodological contribution.

S2: DILITHIUM-2 INFEASIBILITY PROOF
    The TBS feasibility analysis (Section 4.6) rigorously proves Dilithium-2
    is incompatible with SAE J3161 constraints. This analytical result is
    independent of simulation quality and is worth publishing.

S3: SPS GRANT SIZING ANALYSIS
    The observation that SPS grants are sized for the maximum SPDU (cert mode)
    and thus digest-only operation doesn't reduce channel occupancy is a novel
    and important insight for PQC deployment planning.

S4: COMPREHENSIVE METHODOLOGY DESCRIPTION
    Sections 1-4 are well-written with clear problem formulation, thorough
    background, and detailed methodology. The channel model equations, TBS
    lookup procedure, and SPDU structure are precisely documented.

S5: HONEST LIMITATION ACKNOWLEDGMENT
    The paper candidly acknowledges 20 MHz bandwidth deviation, single
    topology, missing one-shot mechanism, and other limitations. This honesty
    is commendable but doesn't substitute for actually fixing the problems.
