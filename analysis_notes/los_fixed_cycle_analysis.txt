1. THE QUESTION
================================================================================

Can we achieve all six LOS levels (A through F) by only changing the vehicle
flow rate in the SUMO route file, without modifying the signal timing?

Short answer: YES — mathematically. d2 grows without bound as X increases
past 1.0, so any target delay is achievable by increasing demand. But there
are practical considerations (see Section 4).


2. FIXED PARAMETERS
================================================================================

From current intersection.net.xml:
  Cycle length:       C = 40s
  Green per phase:    g = 17s
  Yellow per phase:   y = 3s
  Green ratio:        g/C = 17/40 = 0.425

Capacity:
  Saturation flow:    s = 1900 pc/h/ln × 2 lanes = 3800 veh/h
  Capacity:           c = 3800 × 0.425 = 1615 veh/h (through + right)
  Left turn capacity: ~255 veh/h (permitted, opposed)

d1 bounds with C=40s:
  d1_min (X→0):  0.5 × 40 × (0.575)² = 6.61 s/veh
  d1_max (X=1):  6.61 / (1 - 1.0 × 0.425) = 6.61 / 0.575 = 11.50 s/veh

  KEY: d1 is capped at 11.50 s/veh regardless of how much demand increases.
  All additional delay beyond ~11.5s must come from d2.

HCM constants:
  k = 0.50 (pretimed signal)
  I = 1.0 (isolated intersection)
  T = 0.25 h (15-minute analysis period)
  PF = 1.0 (random arrivals, isolated intersection)
  Traffic split: 80% through, 10% left, 10% right

5. OBSERVATIONS
================================================================================

a) YES, it works mathematically. By only changing demand, you can cover all
   six LOS levels with a single 40s cycle.

b) Notice how d1 saturates at 11.5s once X >= 1.0. From LOS D onward,
   d1 is identical (11.5s) — ALL additional delay comes from d2.

c) The demand range is relatively narrow:
     LOS A: 800 veh/h   →   LOS F: 2100 veh/h
   That's a 2.6× range covering the entire LOS spectrum.

d) The transition from LOS B to LOS D is very steep (1500 → 1900 veh/h).
   The intersection is sensitive to demand in the 1500-2000 range because
   capacity is only 1615 veh/h — small changes around X=1.0 produce large
   delay swings.

e) LOS D, E, F all have X > 1.0 (oversaturated). In SUMO, this means:
     - Queues will grow beyond the approach road length
     - Vehicles may not be able to depart (SUMO "teleports" stuck vehicles)
     - The simulation must run long enough for queues to build up
     - Consider extending approach road lengths for LOS E/F to hold queues

   PRACTICAL CONCERN: At 2100 veh/h per approach with capacity of 1615,
   the excess is 485 veh/h. Over a 15-minute period, that's ~121 vehicles
   queuing beyond what the signal can serve. Make sure the SUMO approach
   roads are long enough (at least 500-700m) to hold these queues without
   spillback affecting vehicle generation.


================================================================================
6. ADVANTAGE OF FIXED CYCLE APPROACH
================================================================================

Using a fixed cycle length has a significant benefit for your research:

  - ISOLATES THE DEMAND VARIABLE: All LOS differences come purely from
    traffic volume changes, not signal timing changes. This makes your
    experimental design cleaner.

  - CONTROLS FOR SIGNAL TIMING: In your paper, you can state that signal
    timing is held constant, and only vehicle density varies. This means
    any PQC performance differences across LOS levels are attributable to
    vehicle density (and thus channel congestion), not to different signal
    timing creating different spatial distributions.

  - SIMPLER SUMO SETUP: Only one .net.xml file needed. Just swap .rou.xml
    files for each LOS scenario.
