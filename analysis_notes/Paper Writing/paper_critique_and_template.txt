SECTION B: CRITICAL REVIEW — AS A HOSTILE REVIEWER
================================================================================

I am reviewing this as Reviewer 2 — the tough one. I will be blunt.

Overall assessment: The work addresses a timely and important problem (PQC
migration for V2X), but in its current state, the experimental evidence is
INSUFFICIENT to support several of the claimed contributions. The simulator
infrastructure is solid, but the paper needs significantly more simulation
runs, longer durations, and rigorous statistical treatment before it is
publishable.

Score: MAJOR REVISION (not reject — the foundation is strong, but the
evidence gap is substantial).


─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
STRENGTH 1: REAL CRYPTOGRAPHIC OPERATIONS
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Unlike many V2X security simulation studies that model signatures as fixed
byte arrays with artificial delays, this work integrates actual liboqs and
OpenSSL cryptographic operations. Sign and verify times are measured from
real computations, not assumed from benchmarks. This is a genuine strength.


─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
STRENGTH 2: BUG DISCOVERY AND STANDARDS COMPLIANCE
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

The documentation of 23 bugs (3 critical PHY-layer bugs that invalidated
prior SimuLTE results) is valuable to the community. The SAE J3161
compliance audit is thorough. These are real contributions independent of
the PQC analysis.


─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
STRENGTH 3: DILITHIUM-2 INFEASIBILITY PROOF
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

The analytical proof that Dilithium-2 SPDUs (~3,800 bytes) exceed the
maximum Transport Block Size (2,481 bytes at MCS 11 with 10 subchannels)
is mathematically rigorous and an important result. This alone is worth
reporting.


─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
WEAKNESS 1 (CRITICAL): INSUFFICIENT SIMULATION EVIDENCE
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

The paper claims "multi-scenario comparative analysis" but the evidence
shows:
  - Only BASE scenario results exist with the current (post-bug-fix) config
  - NLOS and High-Density results are from OLD runs (pre-bug-fix, Feb 2026)
    or are analytical PROJECTIONS, not actual simulation
  - Highway scenarios are configured but apparently never executed
  - Simulation duration appears to be ~60 seconds, not the claimed 300s

A reviewer will ask: "How many replications were run? What are the
confidence intervals? Where is the statistical significance test?"

FIX REQUIRED:
  - Run at minimum: 3 algorithms × 3+ vehicle densities × 5 replications
    × 300s each = 45+ simulation runs
  - Report mean ± 95% confidence interval for every metric
  - Use different random seeds for each replication
  - Run post-bug-fix NLOS and high-density scenarios


─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
WEAKNESS 2 (CRITICAL): NO MEANINGFUL PDR DIFFERENCE AT TESTED DENSITY
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

The existing results show:
  ECDSA PDR = 78.4%, Falcon PDR = 77.5% (delta = -0.9%)

At 60 vehicles, the difference is negligible. The analysis itself explains
why: losses are SCI-dominated (control channel), and SCI size is identical
for both algorithms. The larger Falcon TB doesn't cause additional losses.

This is a problem because the paper's THESIS is that PQC packet size
impacts performance. At the tested density, it doesn't.

A reviewer will say: "Your own results show PQC has no meaningful impact
on PDR. Why should I care about this paper?"

FIX REQUIRED:
  - You MUST run high-density scenarios (100, 150, 200 vehicles) where
    resource contention actually manifests
  - The analytical projection (88% resource occupancy at 110 vehicles for
    Falcon vs 22% for ECDSA) is compelling but MUST be validated by actual
    simulation
  - Show the density at which Falcon PDR begins to diverge from ECDSA —
    this is the key result the paper needs


─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
WEAKNESS 3 (HIGH): CBR IS THE STRONGEST ARGUMENT BUT NOT ANALYZED
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

CBR data shows Falcon = 0.212 vs ECDSA = 0.086 (2.5× difference). This
is actually the most compelling result — even when PDR is similar, channel
load is dramatically different. At higher densities, this CBR difference
would trigger DCC and cause PDR divergence.

But CBR is reported as a single average number. No time series, no
distribution, no correlation with vehicle count, no DCC trigger analysis.

FIX REQUIRED:
  - Plot CBR vs time, CBR vs vehicle count
  - Show the density at which Falcon's CBR crosses the 0.30 and 0.65
    DCC thresholds while ECDSA remains below
  - Count DCC packet drops per algorithm
  - This would be the STRONGEST figure in the paper


─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
WEAKNESS 4 (HIGH): NO COMPARISON WITH RELATED WORK RESULTS
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

The paper positions against CHASM (Twardokus & Rahbari, 2025) but does
not compare results quantitatively. CHASM reports specific PDR and latency
numbers for hybrid PQC schemes. This paper should either:
  (a) Reproduce CHASM's scenario and show comparable/better results, or
  (b) Clearly explain why the scenarios differ and what additional insight
      this work provides

A reviewer will ask: "CHASM already studied PQC in V2X. What new
knowledge does this paper add?" The differentiation exists in the notes
but must be made explicit in the paper with side-by-side result comparison.


─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
WEAKNESS 5 (HIGH): CLAIMED ICA CONTRIBUTION NOT DEMONSTRATED
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

The paper claims "V2I + ICA safety application" as a differentiator from
CHASM (which is V2V only). ICA (Intersection Collision Avoidance) code
exists in Mode4RSUApp.cc, but:
  - No ICA-specific PDR or latency results are shown
  - No analysis of ICA warning delivery reliability
  - No comparison of ICA performance under PQC vs ECDSA

If you claim ICA as a contribution, you must show ICA results. Otherwise,
drop the claim.


─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
WEAKNESS 6 (MEDIUM): CERTIFICATE CACHING NOT ANALYZED
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

The implementation supports cert caching (digest mode), which reduces
Falcon SPDU from ~1,739B to ~745B (57% reduction). This is a MAJOR
mitigation strategy for PQC overhead. But:
  - No analysis comparing PDR with cert caching ON vs OFF
  - No analysis of the cert interval parameter's effect
  - "Hashing" and "No_Hashing" result folders exist but are not compared

This is a missed opportunity. Cert caching could be the difference between
"Falcon is feasible" and "Falcon is infeasible at high density."


─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
WEAKNESS 7 (MEDIUM): SINGLE INTERSECTION TOPOLOGY
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

All results are from a single 4-way intersection. The paper should
acknowledge this limitation explicitly. Different topologies (highway,
urban grid, rural) produce different spatial distributions, vehicle
densities, and channel characteristics.

The highway scenarios are configured but not run. Running at least one
highway scenario would significantly strengthen the paper.


─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
WEAKNESS 8 (MEDIUM): MISSING LATENCY ANALYSIS
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

End-to-end latency (delay_ms) is recorded in the CSV but never analyzed.
For safety-critical BSMs, the 100ms latency requirement (SAE J3161) is as
important as PDR. PQC signing and verification times contribute to this
delay. A reviewer will ask: "Do PQC operations cause BSMs to miss the
100ms deadline?"


─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
WEAKNESS 9 (LOW): SPS + ONE-SHOT NOT IMPLEMENTED
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

SAE J3161 Section 7.3.3 mandates one-shot transmission alongside SPS.
This is not implemented. While it doesn't invalidate the PQC comparison
(both algorithms suffer equally), it means the simulation doesn't capture
the full collision avoidance mechanism. A knowledgeable reviewer may flag
this.

Mitigation: Acknowledge as a limitation. State that one-shot would equally
benefit both algorithms and thus doesn't bias the comparison.


─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
WEAKNESS 10 (LOW): NO HARDWARE-IN-THE-LOOP VALIDATION
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

CHASM includes hardware OBU measurements. This work is pure simulation.
While simulation is valid for system-level analysis, a reviewer may argue
that real hardware constraints (CPU scheduling, memory latency, OS jitter)
could affect crypto timing measurements.

Mitigation: Acknowledge. Note that simulation provides controlled,
repeatable conditions and that the crypto operations use real libraries
(not models), giving realistic timing.
