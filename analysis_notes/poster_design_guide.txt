1. RECOMMENDED SECTIONS AND LAYOUT
================================================================================

A research poster is read in 2-3 minutes. Every element must earn its space.

Suggested sections (in reading order, left-to-right or column flow):

  +------------------------------------------------------------------+
  |                     TITLE + AUTHORS + LOGO                        |
  +------------------------------------------------------------------+
  |  INTRODUCTION    |   METHODOLOGY     |   KEY RESULTS (largest)    |
  |  + OBJECTIVE     |                   |                            |
  |                  |                   |                            |
  +------------------+-------------------+----------------------------+
  |  SYSTEM MODEL    |   RESULTS (cont.) |   CONCLUSIONS +            |
  |  (diagram)       |                   |   FUTURE WORK              |
  +------------------+-------------------+----------------------------+
  |                     REFERENCES + QR CODE                          |
  +------------------------------------------------------------------+

Section breakdown:


TITLE BAR (top, full width)
─────────────────────────────
  Title: "Impact of Post-Quantum Cryptographic Signatures on
          LTE-V2X Sidelink Performance for Connected Vehicle Safety Messaging"

  Or shorter: "Can Post-Quantum Signatures Survive the V2X Radio Channel?"

  Authors, affiliation, department, university logo.

  TIP: The title should be readable from 5 feet away. Use 72-96pt font.


SECTION 1: INTRODUCTION + OBJECTIVE (left column, top)
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
  Keep this to 4-5 bullet points maximum. No paragraphs.

  INTRODUCTION:
    - Connected vehicles broadcast Basic Safety Messages (BSMs) 10 times
      per second, digitally signed per IEEE 1609.2
    - Current standard uses ECDSA P-256 (~305 byte signed packets)
    - Quantum computers will break ECDSA — NIST has standardized
      post-quantum replacements (Falcon-512, Dilithium-2)
    - PQC signatures are 10-38x larger — can the V2X radio channel
      handle them?

  OBJECTIVE:
    - Evaluate the feasibility of deploying PQC signature algorithms
      in LTE-V2X (C-V2X) Mode 4 sidelink through standards-compliant
      simulation
    - Measure impact on: Packet Delivery Ratio (PDR), end-to-end latency,
      channel congestion (CBR), and radio resource utilization

  VISUAL: A simple "problem statement" graphic:
    [ECDSA: 305 bytes] --quantum threat--> [Falcon: 1,733 bytes]
    "5.7x larger — does the radio break?"


SECTION 2: SYSTEM MODEL / METHODOLOGY (left or center column)
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
  THIS IS WHERE A DIAGRAM DOES THE HEAVY LIFTING.

  Include ONE system architecture diagram showing:
    - SUMO (vehicle mobility) <-> OMNeT++/SimuLTE (network simulation)
    - Application layer: BSM generation -> IEEE 1609.2 SPDU signing
    - MAC layer: LTE-V2X Mode 4 SPS resource allocation
    - PHY layer: Sidelink transmission on 10 MHz channel

  Below the diagram, a small parameter table:

    Parameter                   Value
    ─────────────────────────   ──────────────
    Scenario                    4-way signalized intersection
    Channel bandwidth           10 MHz (100 RBs)
    Subchannels                 10 (10 RBs each)
    BSM rate                    10 Hz (100ms)
    MCS range                   5-11 (per SAE J3161)
    Congestion control          CBR-based DCC (enabled)
    Vehicle densities tested    800 - 2000 veh/h/approach
    Algorithms                  ECDSA P-256, Falcon-512, Dilithium-2
    Simulation time             300s per run
    Standards                   IEEE 1609.2, SAE J3161, SAE J2735

  TIP: This section should be ~20% of poster area. Don't over-explain
  methodology — the results are what people stop to look at.


SECTION 3: KEY RESULTS (center + right columns — LARGEST section)
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
  This should occupy 40-50% of the poster. Use FIGURES, not text.

  FIGURE 1: SPDU Size Comparison (stacked bar chart)
    Three bars: ECDSA, Falcon-512, Dilithium-2
    Segments: BSM payload (43B), SPDU overhead (28B), signature,
              public key, certificate metadata
    Annotate total sizes. Show Dilithium exceeds max TBS with a
    red dashed line at 2,481 bytes (max transport block size).

    KEY TAKEAWAY CALLOUT BOX:
    "Dilithium-2 SPDUs (3,908 bytes) exceed the maximum transport
     block size — physically impossible to transmit on LTE-V2X sidelink"

  FIGURE 2: PDR vs Vehicle Density (THE key figure)
    Line plot: X = vehicle density, Y = PDR (%)
    Two lines: ECDSA (blue), Falcon-512 (orange)
    Error bars: 95% confidence intervals
    Horizontal dashed line at 90% PDR threshold
    Show where the lines diverge.

  FIGURE 3: CBR vs Vehicle Density (bar or line chart)
    Shows Falcon's 2.5x higher channel load
    Mark the 0.30 and 0.65 DCC threshold lines
    This explains WHY PDR drops for Falcon at high density.

  FIGURE 4 (pick ONE of these):
    Option A: Latency CDF — shows both algorithms meet 100ms deadline
    Option B: Sign/Verify time comparison bar chart
    Option C: Resource utilization (subchannels per TX)

  TIP: Every figure needs a 1-line takeaway printed next to it.
  Reviewers should understand each figure in 5 seconds.


SECTION 4: CONCLUSIONS + FUTURE WORK (right column, bottom)
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
  3-4 bullet conclusions. No hedging — state findings directly:

    1. Dilithium-2 is INFEASIBLE for LTE-V2X: its signed packets
       exceed the physical transmission capacity of the sidelink.

    2. Falcon-512 is FEASIBLE at low-to-moderate vehicle densities
       but consumes 3.5x more radio resources per BSM, causing
       [X]% PDR reduction at [Y] vehicle density compared to ECDSA.

    3. Channel congestion (CBR) is the primary bottleneck: Falcon's
       larger packets push CBR past DCC thresholds sooner, triggering
       packet drops at lower vehicle densities than ECDSA.

    4. Certificate caching (digest mode) reduces Falcon overhead by
       57% (1,733B -> 745B) and is essential for practical deployment.

  FUTURE WORK (2 bullets max):
    - Implement hybrid ECDSA+PQC schemes (cf. CHASM) to evaluate
      transition strategies
    - Evaluate Falcon-512 under NR-V2X (5G sidelink) with wider
      bandwidth


SECTION 5: REFERENCES + CONTACT (bottom strip, full width)
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
  4-5 key references only:
    [1] IEEE Std 1609.2-2022
    [2] SAE J3161/1 (JUL 2024)
    [3] Twardokus & Rahbari, "CHASM," NDSS 2025
    [4] NIST FIPS 204 (ML-DSA / Dilithium)
    [5] NIST FIPS 206 (FN-DSA / Falcon)

  Add a QR code linking to your code repo or preprint (if available).


================================================================================
2. DESIGN TIPS
================================================================================

LAYOUT:
  - Use 3-column layout (most readable for large posters)
  - Standard poster size: 48" x 36" (landscape) or 36" x 48" (portrait)
  - Check your competition's required dimensions
  - White space is your friend — don't fill every inch

FONTS:
  - Title: 72-96pt, bold, sans-serif (Arial, Helvetica, Calibri)
  - Section headers: 36-44pt, bold
  - Body text: 24-28pt
  - Figure captions: 20-24pt
  - Rule of thumb: readable from 4-5 feet away

COLORS:
  - Pick 2-3 colors maximum for data (e.g., blue=ECDSA, orange=Falcon,
    red=Dilithium)
  - Use the same colors consistently across ALL figures
  - Dark text on light background (never reverse for body text)
  - Use your university's brand colors for the header/border

FIGURES:
  - Generate at 300 DPI minimum (use dpi=300 in matplotlib savefig)
  - Large axis labels (16pt+ in matplotlib before export)
  - Remove chart junk: no gridlines on bar charts, minimal tick marks
  - Every figure should have a 1-line bold takeaway above or below it

TEXT:
  - NEVER use paragraphs on a poster. Bullet points only.
  - Total word count: 300-500 words (excluding figure labels)
  - If you can't explain it in a bullet, it doesn't belong on the poster

TOOLS:
  - PowerPoint: Easiest, most flexible. Set slide size to poster dimensions.
  - LaTeX (beamerposter): Professional look, steep learning curve.
    Overleaf template: search "beamerposter" on Overleaf.
  - Canva: Good for non-LaTeX users who want modern design.
  - Figma: Best for precise layout control.

  Recommendation: PowerPoint is fine for a poster competition. Don't
  overthink the tool — spend time on the CONTENT and FIGURES.


================================================================================
3. WHAT JUDGES LOOK FOR
================================================================================

  1. CLARITY: Can I understand the problem and result in 60 seconds?
  2. VISUAL IMPACT: Do the figures tell the story without reading text?
  3. NOVELTY: Is this solving a real problem? (Yes — quantum threat to V2X)
  4. RIGOR: Are there error bars? Multiple scenarios? Standards references?
  5. PRESENTATION: Is the layout clean, professional, not cluttered?

  Common mistakes to avoid:
    - Too much text (wall of text = nobody reads it)
    - Tiny fonts (if they squint, you lose them)
    - Too many figures (4-5 max, each one must be essential)
    - No clear takeaway (every section needs a "so what?")
    - Missing units on axes


================================================================================
4. SUGGESTED FLOW FOR YOUR POSTER TALK (2-3 MINUTE PITCH)
================================================================================

When someone stops at your poster, use this flow:

  [10s] "Connected vehicles sign safety messages using ECDSA. Quantum
         computers will break that. We need post-quantum replacements."

  [10s] "The problem: PQC signatures are 5-10x larger. Can the V2X
         radio channel handle them?"

  [15s] Point to SPDU size figure: "Dilithium-2 packets are too large
         to even transmit — they exceed the physical limit. Falcon-512
         fits, but uses 3.5x more radio resources."

  [20s] Point to PDR vs Density figure: "At low density both work fine.
         But as we add more vehicles, Falcon's larger packets cause
         channel congestion and packet drops start earlier."

  [15s] Point to CBR figure: "This is why — Falcon pushes channel
         load 2.5x higher, triggering congestion control sooner."

  [10s] "Conclusion: Falcon-512 is feasible but requires careful
         congestion management. Dilithium-2 is not viable for V2X.
         Certificate caching cuts Falcon's overhead by 57%."

  [10s] "Questions?"

Total: ~90 seconds. Leave time for questions.
