PDR METHODOLOGY COMPARISON: BIN-FIRST (v2) vs 5GAA SLIDING WINDOW
===================================================================
Date: 2026-05-05


DIFFERENCE BETWEEN THE TWO SCRIPTS
====================================

Script v2 (bin-first approach)
--------------------------------
1. Takes all received packets and immediately assigns each one to a distance
   bin based on its dist_m value.
2. Groups by (sender, receiver, bin_idx).
3. For each group, computes span = max(msgId) - min(msgId) + 1 and
   PDR = received / span.
4. The denominator is estimated from however many packets happened to fall
   inside that spatial slice.

The core problem: a vehicle moving at 30-50 km/h crosses a 30m bin in roughly
2-3 seconds, producing only 20-30 packets per bin in ideal conditions, and far
fewer if the bin is at a boundary. Sparse bins produce unstable, noisy PDR
estimates. A span=1 observation always yields 100% regardless of reality.


Script 5GAA (time-window approach)
------------------------------------
1. For each (sender, receiver) pair, sorts packets by time.
2. For each received packet at timestamp t and distance d:
   - Opens a 5-second window [t - 2.5s, t + 2.5s] in time.
   - Counts all received packets inside that window and computes their msgId span.
   - PRR = received_in_window / span * 100.
   - Records (d, PRR) as one scatter point.
3. After collecting all scatter points across all pairs, bins them by distance
   for plotting.

The window always covers approximately 50 packets (5 seconds at 10 Hz), making
every PRR estimate statistically stable before any binning happens. The distance
bin is only used for aggregation at the end, not for computing the PRR itself.


HOW THE 5GAA APPROACH WORKS IN DETAIL
=======================================

Step 1: Sort by time per link
------------------------------
For each (sender, receiver) pair, all received packets are sorted ascending by
timestamp t. This gives a time series of receptions for that specific link.


Step 2: Slide the window over every received packet
----------------------------------------------------
For each packet at position idx in the sorted series, the algorithm defines a
centered 5-second observation window. The window boundaries are found using
binary search (searchsorted), which is fast even for large arrays.

    window = all packets where t_center - 2.5 <= t <= t_center + 2.5

This window is time-continuous. It overlaps heavily with neighboring windows
(the next packet is only 100ms later), so the PRR curve is smooth by
construction.


Step 3: Compute PRR inside the window
---------------------------------------
Inside the window there are approximately 50 received packets (at 10 Hz over
5 seconds, assuming no loss). The msgId span of these 50 packets is
approximately 50 as well, because msgId increments by 1 per transmission
attempt at the sender, independent of whether the packet was received. This
makes the span a reliable proxy for sent count over a 5-second window, unlike
over a sparse spatial slice where it could be as small as 1.

    span = max(msgId in window) - min(msgId in window) + 1   (approx 50)
    PRR  = len(window) / span * 100

If 45 of 50 packets were received, PRR = 90%. If 30 were received, PRR = 60%.
The estimate is stable because the denominator is large and the numerator
cannot exceed it.


Step 4: Record the scatter point at the packet's distance
----------------------------------------------------------
The PRR just computed is tagged with the distance dist_m of the center packet,
not the average distance of the window. This is exactly what the 5GAA report
describes in Section 5.2.4 of 5GAA P-190033:

    "Each PER value associated with a time window is plotted against the
     distance of the vehicle at the center of that window."

So every packet in the dataset generates one (dist_m, PRR) scatter point,
provided the window had at least MIN_PKTS received packets.


Step 5: Bin the scatter by distance
-------------------------------------
After collecting thousands of (dist_m, PRR) scatter points across all links
and all time steps, the points are grouped into 30m distance bins. Within each
bin, the mean, median, and full distribution of PRR values are computed and
plotted as a box plot with lines.


Why this produces the downward parabola
----------------------------------------
A vehicle near the RSU (say, at 50m) consistently experiences a strong signal
throughout every 5-second window centered on its packets. Most windows produce
PRR close to 100%. A vehicle at 400m consistently experiences weaker signal and
more SPS collisions relative to its distance. Its windows produce lower PRR.

Because each PRR estimate already integrates 5 seconds of physics before
binning, the bin-level statistics reflect the true channel quality at that
distance, not the noise of sparse spatial sampling. The result is a smooth,
monotonically decreasing curve that matches the physics and the literature.


THE ONE REMAINING DIFFERENCE FROM THE ORIGINAL 5GAA TEST
==========================================================
The 5GAA field test has a Tx log: the transmitting vehicle records every packet
it queued for transmission, with its exact timestamp and sequence number. Their
denominator is the true sent count, not the msgId span.

We do not have a Tx log. We use the msgId span within the window as a proxy.
Over 5 seconds at 10 Hz, this approximation introduces at most 2 packets of
error at the window edges (one at the leading edge, one at the trailing edge),
giving a denominator error of roughly 4% in the worst case. This is small
enough to have negligible effect on the shape of the curve.
